Question 1The matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix} i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & i & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ is (CO1)A. Hermitian matrix onlyB. Skew - Hermitian matrix onlyC. Hermitian and unitary matrix bothD. Skew - Hermitian and unitary matrix both.Detail Solution 1Let the given matrix be $A = \begin{bmatrix} i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & i & 0 \end{bmatrix}$.Check for Hermitian/Skew-Hermitian:A matrix $A$ is Hermitian if $A^{\theta} = A$, and Skew-Hermitian if $A^{\theta} = -A$.(Where $A^{\theta} = (\overline{A})^T$, the conjugate transpose).Conjugate of A ($\overline{A}$):$$\overline{A} = \begin{bmatrix} \overline{i} & \overline{0} & \overline{0} \\ \overline{0} & \overline{0} & \overline{i} \\ \overline{0} & \overline{i} & \overline{0} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -i \\ 0 & -i & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$Conjugate Transpose of A ($A^{\theta}$):$$A^{\theta} = (\overline{A})^T = \begin{bmatrix} -i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -i \\ 0 & -i & 0 \end{bmatrix}^T = \begin{bmatrix} -i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -i \\ 0 & -i & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$Comparison:$$A^{\theta} = \begin{bmatrix} -i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -i \\ 0 & -i & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$$$-A = -\begin{bmatrix} i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & i & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -i \\ 0 & -i & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$Since $A^{\theta} = -A$, the matrix $A$ is a Skew-Hermitian matrix.Check for Unitary:A matrix $A$ is Unitary if $A A^{\theta} = I$ (Identity matrix).Calculate $A A^{\theta}$:$$A A^{\theta} = \begin{bmatrix} i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & i \\ 0 & i & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -i & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -i \\ 0 & -i & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$$(1,1)$ element: $(i)(-i) + (0)(0) + (0)(0) = -i^2 = -(-1) = 1$$(2,2)$ element: $(0)(0) + (0)(0) + (i)(-i) = -i^2 = 1$$(3,3)$ element: $(0)(0) + (i)(-i) + (0)(0) = -i^2 = 1$$(1,2)$ element: $(i)(0) + (0)(0) + (0)(-i) = 0$$(2,3)$ element: $(0)(0) + (0)(-i) + (i)(0) = 0$... and so on.$$A A^{\theta} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I$$Since $A A^{\theta} = I$, the matrix $A$ is a Unitary matrix.Conclusion: The matrix $A$ is both Skew-Hermitian and Unitary.Correct Option: D. Skew - Hermitian and unitary matrix both.Question 2By applying elementary transformations to a matrix, its rank is (CO1)A. IncreasesB. DecreasesC. Does not changeD. None of these.Detail Solution 2Elementary transformations (or elementary row/column operations) are operations used to simplify a matrix, often to bring it to Echelon form. These operations do not change the linear dependency between the rows or columns.The Rank of a matrix is defined as:The number of non-zero rows in the Echelon form, ORThe maximum number of linearly independent rows (or columns).Since elementary transformations do not alter the linear independence property of the rows/columns, the rank of the matrix remains the same.Correct Option: C. Does not change.Question 3The characteristic equation of the matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ is (CO1)A. $-\lambda^3 +\lambda^2 +\lambda-1=0$B. $-\lambda^3 +\lambda^2 -\lambda-1=0$C. $-\lambda^3 -\lambda^2 +\lambda-1=0$D. $-\lambda^3 +\lambda^2 +\lambda+1=0$.Detail Solution 3The characteristic equation of a matrix $A$ is given by $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$, where $I$ is the identity matrix and $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue.$$A - \lambda I = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} - \lambda \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1-\lambda & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0-\lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0-\lambda \end{bmatrix}$$Now, calculate the determinant $\det(A - \lambda I)$:$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (1-\lambda) \cdot \det\begin{bmatrix} -\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & -\lambda \end{bmatrix} - 0 \cdot (\dots) + 0 \cdot (\dots)$$$$ = (1-\lambda) \cdot [ (-\lambda)(-\lambda) - (1)(1) ]$$$$ = (1-\lambda) \cdot (\lambda^2 - 1)$$$$ = (1-\lambda) \cdot (\lambda - 1) (\lambda + 1)$$$$ = -(\lambda - 1) (\lambda - 1) (\lambda + 1)$$$$ = -(\lambda^2 - 2\lambda + 1) (\lambda + 1)$$Alternatively, expanding $(1-\lambda)(\lambda^2 - 1)$:$$ = 1(\lambda^2 - 1) - \lambda(\lambda^2 - 1)$$$$ = \lambda^2 - 1 - \lambda^3 + \lambda$$Setting the determinant to zero:$$\lambda^2 - 1 - \lambda^3 + \lambda = 0$$Multiplying by $-1$ to match the options (which start with $-\lambda^3$):$$\lambda^3 - \lambda^2 - \lambda + 1 = 0$$The characteristic equation is $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$, which is $-\lambda^3 + \lambda^2 + \lambda - 1 = 0$ (by multiplying the standard form $\lambda^3 - \lambda^2 - \lambda + 1 = 0$ by $-1$).Correct Option: A. $-\lambda^3 +\lambda^2 +\lambda-1=0$.Question 4If A be an $n$-rowed non-singular matrix, $X$ be an $n \times 1$ matrix, $B$ be $n \times 1$ matrix, the system of equations, $A X = B$, has (CO1)A. Unique solutionB. Infinite solutionC. More than two solutionsD. None of these.Detail Solution 4The given system of linear equations is $A X = B$.Non-singular Matrix: A matrix $A$ is non-singular if its determinant is non-zero, i.e., $\det(A) \neq 0$.Inverse Exists: If $A$ is non-singular, its inverse, $A^{-1}$, exists.Rank Condition: For a non-singular matrix of order $n$, the rank of $A$ is $n$.Since $A$ is $n \times n$, $\text{Rank}(A) = n$.The augmented matrix $[A:B]$ will also have $\text{Rank}([A:B]) = n$.The number of unknowns is also $n$ (since $X$ is $n \times 1$).Consistency Rule: The system $A X = B$ is consistent (has a solution) if $\text{Rank}(A) = \text{Rank}([A:B])$.Here, $\text{Rank}(A) = n$ and $\text{Rank}([A:B]) = n$. So, it is consistent.Uniqueness Rule:If $\text{Rank}(A) = \text{Rank}([A:B]) = \text{Number of unknowns} (n)$, then the system has a Unique Solution.If the ranks are equal but less than $n$, the system has Infinite Solutions.Alternatively, since $A^{-1}$ exists, we can multiply $A X = B$ by $A^{-1}$ from the left:$$A^{-1}(A X) = A^{-1} B$$$$(A^{-1} A) X = A^{-1} B$$$$I X = A^{-1} B$$$$X = A^{-1} B$$Since $A^{-1} B$ is a unique matrix product, the solution $X$ is unique.Correct Option: A. Unique solution.Question 5If every minor of order $r$ of a matrix $A$ is zero, then rank of $A$ is (CO1)A. Greater than $r$B. Equal to $r$C. Less than or equal to $r$D. Less than $r$Detail Solution 5The Rank of a matrix $A$, denoted by $\text{Rank}(A)$, is defined as the largest order $r$ of a non-zero minor of the matrix $A$.If $\text{Rank}(A) = k$, it means there is at least one non-zero minor of order $k$, AND every minor of order $(k+1)$ and higher is zero.The question states that every minor of order $r$ of matrix $A$ is zero.This implies that $\text{Rank}(A)$ cannot be $r$, nor can it be greater than $r$. (Because if $\text{Rank}(A) \ge r$, there must be at least one non-zero minor of order $r$ or higher).Since every minor of order $r$ is zero, the largest order of a non-zero minor must be less than $r$.Therefore, the $\text{Rank}(A)$ is less than $r$ (i.e., $\text{Rank}(A) \le r-1$).Correct Option: D. Less than $r$.
